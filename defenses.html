Defenses Against Transposable Elements

Methylation

One common way DNA replication is repressed is methylation. Methylation can occur by adding a methyl group to cytosine or adenine. Once a section of DNA is methylated, the cell acts differently on that section, often not by expressing it. The expression of transposons is usually what causes their duplication, so a methylating transposon is often a good cellular strategy for containing them.

The transposon family Alu accounts for ~10% of human DNA. Alus also contain 9 times more C followed by G sequences than human DNA in general. Methylation almost always occurs on a C followed by a G. As a result, Alu genes account for 33% of potential methylation sites and are almost totally methylated in human cells.[1]

RNA Interference Pathway

An ancient but still active cellular mechanism is the RNA Interference Pathway (or RNAi). RNAi serves an immune system targeting viruses and transposons by identifying harmful RNA and destroying it.

The cell produces small sequences of RNA called small interfering RNAs (siRNAs). These siRNAs join with a RNAi-Induced Silencing Complex (RISC) which then targets and destroys specific mRNA sequences. In this way, RISC is similar to a CRISPR system but for RNA.

The RNA Interference Pathway has been studied primarily in fruit flies (Drosophila) and plans, but has also been found in mammals.[2]
